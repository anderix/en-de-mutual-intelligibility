# Suffix -bar

German *-bar* attaches to verbs to form adjectives. It maps to English *-able* (or sometimes *-ible*) most of the time, and to English *-ful* in a few legacy cases.

The etymological connection: German *-bar* descends from Old High German *-bāri* meaning "carrying" or "bearing" (related to English *bear*). English *-able* is from Latin *-abilis*. Different origins; same semantic role.

## Pairs

**wonderful ↔ wunderbar** *(A1, from Wunder)*
This pair maps to *-ful* rather than *-able*. *Wonder + ful = wunder + bar*.
*Der Kuchen schmeckt wunderbar.*

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

The *-bar/-able* mapping is fully productive:
*essbar / edible*, *trinkbar / drinkable*, *erkennbar / recognizable*, *machbar / doable / makeable*, *lesbar / readable*, *hörbar / audible / hearable*, *sichtbar / visible*, *unsichtbar / invisible*, *unbestreitbar / indisputable*, *vorstellbar / imaginable / conceivable*, *erreichbar / reachable*, *vermeidbar / avoidable*, *anwendbar / applicable*.

The *-ful* mapping is much smaller and historical:
*dankbar / thankful*, *fruchtbar / fruitful*, *furchtbar / fearful (terrible)*, *sorgenvoll* uses *-voll* for "full of."

## Note

The English suffix *-able* maps to two German suffixes: *-bar* (productive, common) and *-lich* (which sometimes overlaps for older words like *möglich / possible*). When in doubt, *-bar* is the safer guess for a coined or technical adjective; *-lich* is more common in older or higher-frequency vocabulary.
