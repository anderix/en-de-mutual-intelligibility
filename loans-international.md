# Other international loans

Words shared between English and German that came from neither language and aren't strictly Latin or Greek. Sources include Arabic, French (non-Latin route), Italian (non-Latin route), Slavic languages, Yiddish, Asian languages, and indigenous Americas.

A1 yield is sparse, mostly through Arabic-via-Romance routes and a few Asian imports. The category will grow at higher levels, especially as we hit cultural, culinary, and political vocabulary.

## Pairs

### From Arabic (often via Spanish, Italian, or French)

**coffee ↔ der Kaffee** *(A1)*
Arabic *qahwa* → Turkish *kahve* → Italian *caffè* → both English and German.
*Ein Kaffee am Morgen macht mich wach.*

**sofa ↔ das Sofa** *(A1)*
Arabic *suffah* → Turkish → Romance → both languages.
*Das Sofa im Wohnzimmer ist sehr bequem.*

### From Chinese (via maritime trade)

**tea ↔ der Tee** *(A1)*
Min Chinese *t'e* → Dutch *thee* → both English *tea* and German *Tee*. Identical sound.
*Am Nachmittag trinke ich gern Tee.*

### From French (post-Norman, non-Latin route)

**balcony ↔ der Balkon** *(A1)*
French *balcon* (originally from Italian *balcone*).

**hotel ↔ das Hotel** *(A1)*
French *hôtel* → both languages.

**office (Büro) ↔ das Büro** *(A1, in compounds: Reisebüro)*
French *bureau*.

**toilet ↔ die Toilette** *(A1)*
French *toilette*.

**restaurant ↔ das Restaurant** *(A1)*
French *restaurant*. German often pronounces it French-style.

### From Spanish via Nahuatl

**tomato ↔ die Tomate** *(A1)*
Nahuatl *tomatl* → Spanish *tomate* → both languages.

### Family vocabulary (Italian/cross-Romance)

**Oma, Opa ↔ die Oma, der Opa** *(A1)*
Less clearly traced. Both languages use these as informal grandma/grandpa terms; English borrowed *opa* in some communities. The diminutive pattern is Romance-influenced but the words themselves are partly nursery-language.

### A2 additions

### From Italian (beyond Latin scholarly)

**pizza ↔ die Pizza** *(A2)*
Direct Italian loan in both languages.
*Eine Pizza mit viel Käse, bitte.*

**account ↔ das Konto** *(A2, Italian via Latin)*
From Italian *conto*.
*Ich zahle das Geld auf mein Konto ein.*

### From Hindi/Urdu via English

**jeans ↔ die Jeans** *(A2)*
Originally Genoa (Italian *Genova*) via French *jean* → eventually standardized through American English.

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

The international loan layer expands rapidly above A1:

**Arabic-origin** that both languages share:
*Algorithmus / algorithm, Algebra / algebra, Magazin / magazine, Tariff / Tarif, Zucker / sugar* (less obvious — different paths), *Cotton / Baumwolle* (English kept Arabic, German used native), *Admiral / Admiral, Alkohol / alcohol*.

**Italian-origin** beyond Latin scholarly:
*Pizza, Spaghetti, Espresso, Cappuccino, Konto / account* (both from Italian *conto*), *Bank / bank* (both from *banca*), *Firma / firm* (both from Italian).

**Slavic-origin** loans (mostly culture-specific, sparse but recognizable):
*Wodka / vodka* (Russian), *Pogrom / pogrom* (Russian), *Steppe / steppe* (Russian), *Tundra / tundra* (Sami via Russian), *Sputnik / sputnik* (Russian), *Roboter / robot* (Czech via Karel Čapek), *Mazurka / mazurka* (Polish dance), *Polka / polka* (Czech).

**Yiddish-origin** in both English and German:
*Mensch* (already in A1), *Chuzpe / chutzpah, Ganove / ganef, Tacheles* (both languages use it for plain talk).

**Japanese-origin**:
*Tsunami, Karate, Sushi, Manga, Anime, Origami, Bonsai, Karaoke, Kimono*. Most are post-1950 imports into both.

**Indigenous American** (via Spanish or English to German):
*Tomate / tomato* (already in A1), *Kakao / cocoa, Tabak / tobacco, Mais / maize, Kanu / canoe, Hängematte / hammock, Kojote / coyote*.

**Hindi/Urdu-origin** (via British English to German):
*Bungalow, Veranda, Pyjama / pyjamas, Khaki, Kaschmir / cashmere, Curry, Shampoo*.

## Watch list

We are on the lookout for Slavic borrowings specifically. The yield is real but limited — Slavic loans tend to be culture-specific and concentrate at C1 and above. The clearest watchlist items: *Wodka, Pogrom, Steppe, Tundra, Roboter, Polka, Mazurka, Sputnik, Dacha* (less common in German), *Borschtsch* (borscht).

## Note

This file's job is to keep visible the fact that English-German mutual intelligibility extends well beyond their shared Germanic roots and shared Latin/Greek scholarly layer. Both languages are heavy borrowers, and they often borrow from the same sources at similar times, leaving us with a third layer of accidental but genuine cross-recognition.
