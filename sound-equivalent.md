# Sound-equivalent (different spelling)

Words that sound nearly identical in English and German, but are spelled differently. Madrigal underweights this category because she organized her book by spelling. We give it proper weight: a learner's ear catches these instantly even when the page does not.

A1 yields a small set, partly because Germanic-rooted vocabulary tends to have shifted *both* spelling and sound. The category will grow at higher levels as more loan words and sibling words appear.

## Pairs

**house ↔ das Haus** *(A1)*
Both pronounced /haʊs/. Germanic root, same sound, different spelling (English *ou*, German *au*). The *au/ou* alternation is consistent — see also *Maus/mouse* below.
*Ihr Haus steht direkt am Wald.*

**mouse ↔ die Maus** *(A1 scope)*
Both pronounced /maʊs/.
*Eine kleine Maus läuft durch die Küche.*

**tea ↔ der Tee** *(A1)*
Both pronounced approximately /teː/. Both languages got the word from Chinese via Dutch trade.
*Am Nachmittag trinke ich gern Tee.*

**sea ↔ die See** *(A1, in compound)*
The simple noun is in A1 in compounds (am Meer, am See). German *See* (feminine) means "sea"; *See* (masculine) means "lake." The sound matches English /siː/ closely.

**eye ↔ das Auge** *(A1, partial)*
Both have the /aɪ/ diphthong, though German *Auge* extends with a final schwa: "OW-guh" vs English "eye" /aɪ/. Less perfect than the *Haus/house* match but the diphthong correspondence is what makes it recognizable.
*Sie hat blaue Augen wie ihre Mutter.*

**ice ↔ das Eis** *(A2)*
Both pronounced /aɪs/. German *Eis* also means "ice cream" in everyday usage.
*Im Sommer esse ich gern ein Eis.*

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

*ice → Eis* (both /aɪs/), *house → Haus* and *mouse → Maus* extend a pattern, *south → Süd* (less close), *cow → Kuh* (close in vowel).

The big batch will come from words borrowed from the same source:
*tomato → Tomate*, *banana → Banane*, *pizza → Pizza*, *coffee → Kaffee* (sound-close), *butter → Butter* (already in identical-spelling).

## Note: the au / ou correspondence

The English diphthong *ou* (as in *house, mouse, out, sound*) corresponds to German *au* (as in *Haus, Maus, aus*). The full pattern: any English Germanic word with *ou* /aʊ/ likely has a German cognate with *au*. *House/Haus, mouse/Maus, louse/Laus, out/aus, sour/sauer, thousand/tausend* (also shows th→d).

This is one of the cleaner "different spelling, same sound" patterns. Worth surfacing as its own micro-rule.
