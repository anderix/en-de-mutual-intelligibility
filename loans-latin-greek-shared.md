# Latin and Greek loans shared by English and German

The largest single source of mutual intelligibility above basic vocabulary. Both languages absorbed a vast scholarly, technical, and cultural vocabulary from Latin and Greek (often via French as the intermediary). The German spellings are usually Germanized slightly — capitalized as nouns, sometimes with *k* for *c* or *z* for *t* — but the words remain transparent.

This file already shows substantial yield at A1 and will explode at higher levels. The Madrigal book builds entire chapters on this category.

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

A1 already gives ~60 entries. A2 will roughly double this. B1 explodes it. The major categories at higher levels:
- Sciences and academic terms (*Mathematik, Physik, Biologie, Geographie, Chemie, Philosophie*)
- Politics and society (*Demokratie, Republik, Politik, Bürokratie, Konflikt*)
- Arts and culture (*Musik, Theater, Konzert, Konzept, Kritik, Komödie, Tragödie*)
- Religion (*Religion, Christentum, Tradition, Symbol*)
- Law and abstract concepts (*Justiz, Argument, Diskussion, Konsequenz, Resultat, Theorie*)

## Note

This file is the strongest argument against the assumption that "German is hard." A B1-level English speaker who is willing to mentally Germanize the spelling can read German newspaper headlines and academic abstracts at substantial comprehension purely on shared vocabulary, before they've learned a single sound shift.
