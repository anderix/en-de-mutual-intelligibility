# Warning: pseudo-anglicisms (Scheinanglizismen)

German words that look English but aren't used that way by English speakers. They sit in the gap where German felt like it was borrowing from English but invented or repurposed something English speakers wouldn't recognize.

This category is almost entirely post-1945, and most entries are post-1980. A1 yields just one famous example.

## Entries from A1

**Handy ↔ "cell phone" (NOT "handy / useful")** *(A1, das Handy)*
The single most cited pseudo-anglicism. *Handy* in English is an adjective meaning convenient or useful. In German, *das Handy* is a noun meaning mobile phone, derived loosely from English *handy* but used in a way no English speaker would understand without explanation.
*Mein Handy ist leider zu Hause geblieben.* (My **cell phone** unfortunately stayed at home.)
For "handy" in the English sense, German uses *praktisch* or *nützlich*.

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

The famous pseudo-anglicism set comes online at A2 and B1:

**Beamer ↔ "projector"** (NOT a BMW). German uses *Beamer* for video projectors. In English, *Beemer* is informal for a BMW.

**Smoking ↔ "tuxedo"** (NOT smoking the activity). German *der Smoking* is a tuxedo or dinner jacket. *Smoking* the gerund is *das Rauchen*.

**Oldtimer ↔ "classic / vintage car"** (NOT an old person). In German, *der Oldtimer* is specifically a vintage car. In English, "old-timer" refers to a person.

**Public Viewing ↔ "outdoor screening / watch party"** (in English, "public viewing" most often refers to a viewing of a corpse before a funeral). German uses it for crowds gathered to watch sports or events on big outdoor screens. The English connotation is jarring.

**Shooting ↔ "photo shoot"** (NOT a violent shooting). German *das Shooting* is a photo session.

**Mobbing ↔ "bullying"** (NOT a mob attack). *Mobbing* in German is workplace or school bullying.

**Home Office ↔ "working from home / remote work"** (NOT the British government department). German *Home Office* (or *Homeoffice*) is the practice of working from home. In English (especially UK), *the Home Office* is a specific government ministry.

**Peeling ↔ "exfoliating scrub / facial scrub"** (NOT skin coming off). German *das Peeling* is a cosmetic exfoliant.

**Showmaster ↔ "TV show host"** (NOT recognized in English). German *der Showmaster* is a host or presenter; English uses *host* or *presenter*.

**Trampen ↔ "to hitchhike"** (NOT tramping in the British sense of hiking). German *trampen* is hitchhiking.

**Wellness ↔ "spa, health/relaxation services"** (in English, *wellness* is a broader concept of health). German *Wellness* commercially refers to spa services and relaxation packages.

**Bodybag ↔ "messenger bag / shoulder bag"** (NOT a bag for corpses). German *der Bodybag* is a fashion accessory; the English meaning is morbid.

**Streetworker ↔ "social worker who works on the street"** (NOT a sex worker). German *Streetworker* is the social-work usage; the English connotation is very different.

## Note

These create genuine intercultural friction. A German speaker proudly mentioning *Public Viewing* for a soccer match, or asking for a *Bodybag* in a fashion store, will baffle or alarm English speakers. The reverse trap also exists: English speakers using "handy" or "smoking" in German get the wrong meaning across.

For learners, the rule is: when you see an English-looking word in German, don't assume it carries the English meaning. Check it against this file or the Duden. Most pseudo-anglicisms are commercial or media coinages from the 1960s-2000s and have no English-side equivalent at all.
