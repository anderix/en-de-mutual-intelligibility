# Suffix -ität

German *-ität* maps directly to English *-ity*. Both descend from the Latin suffix *-itas* / *-itatem* for abstract nouns derived from adjectives. The spelling is close — German adds an umlaut to the *a* — and the meaning is preserved almost perfectly across a huge class of words.

All *-ität* nouns are feminine in German (*die*).

This pattern was foreshadowed at A2 by *Qualität* and *Universität*. At B1 it opens up, and at B2-C1 it becomes one of the single highest-yield rules a learner can internalize.

## Pairs

**quality ↔ die Qualität** *(A2)*
*Die Qualität dieses Produkts ist ausgezeichnet.*

**university ↔ die Universität** *(A2)*
*Meine Tochter studiert an der Universität Berlin.*

**activity ↔ die Aktivität** *(B1)*
*Am Wochenende machen wir viele Aktivitäten mit den Kindern.*

**mobility ↔ die Mobilität** *(B1)*
*Die Mobilität in der Stadt ist sehr wichtig.*

**reality ↔ die Realität** *(B1)*
*Die Realität ist oft anders als unsere Vorstellung.*

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

At B2 and C1 the pattern pays out massively across abstract and academic vocabulary:
*Identität / identity*, *Nationalität / nationality*, *Priorität / priority*, *Aktivität / activity* (already above), *Kreativität / creativity*, *Produktivität / productivity*, *Autorität / authority*, *Popularität / popularity*, *Elektrizität / electricity*, *Intensität / intensity*, *Spezialität / specialty*, *Mentalität / mentality*, *Solidarität / solidarity*, *Universität / university* (above), *Mehrheit* (uses *-heit* instead), *Minorität* or *Minderheit*, *Kapazität / capacity*, *Integrität / integrity*, *Kontinuität / continuity*, *Diversität / diversity*, *Flexibilität / flexibility*, *Sensibilität / sensibility* (careful: *sensibel* is a false friend — see warning-false-friends.md).

## Note

The transformation is almost mechanical:
- English *-ity* → German *-ität*
- Add umlaut over *a*
- Noun is feminine (*die*)
- Stress falls on the final syllable in German (*Qua-li-TÄT*), not where English puts it (*QUA-li-ty*)

If you know the English word, you can nearly always produce the German equivalent. This is the single best reason to front-load vocabulary exposure to Latin-rooted academic English when preparing to read German at B2 and above.

## Related

The *-heit* and *-keit* suffixes form another huge family of abstract feminine nouns (*Freiheit / freedom, Krankheit / illness, Möglichkeit / possibility*). These don't share a direct Latin root with English, but they function identically within German and often translate to English *-ness*, *-dom*, or *-ity*. Worth its own file at B1 when density grows.
