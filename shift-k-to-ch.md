# k → ch

The High German consonant shift turned Proto-Germanic */k/* into German */x/* (the *ch* sound, like Scottish "loch") between or after vowels. English kept the older */k/*. Word-initial *k* mostly stayed as *k* in both languages.

## Pairs

**make ↔ machen** *(A1, verb)*
*Was machst du am Wochenende?*

**book ↔ das Buch** *(A1)*
*Das Buch ist sehr spannend.*

**milk ↔ die Milch** *(A1)*
*Die Milch steht im Kühlschrank.*

**kitchen ↔ die Küche** *(A1)*
Word-initial *k* preserved on both sides; medial *k* → *ch* in German.
*Wir essen meistens in der Küche.*

**seek ↔ suchen** *(A1, verb)*
Final *k* in English → *ch* in German. Modern English uses *seek* less than *search*; the cognate is the older form.
*Ich suche meine Brille.*

**laugh ↔ lachen** *(A1, verb)*
English *gh* in *laugh* was historically /x/, the same sound German still spells *ch*. So *lachen / laugh* are the same etymological word with English having silently mutated /x/ → /f/. See also shift-gh-to-ch.md.
*Das Baby lacht sehr oft.*

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

*break → brechen*, *speak → sprechen* (already partial in A1: *sprechen*), *week → Woche*, *rich → reich*, *teach → zeigen* (irregular), *wake → wachen*, *I → ich* (high-frequency irregular), *not → nicht* (high-frequency irregular).

## Note

Two A1 function words — *ich* (I) and *nicht* (not) — also show this shift, but in irregular form. Worth treating as memorized rather than rule-derived.
