# t → ss / ß / z

The High German consonant shift turned Proto-Germanic */t/* into German */s/* or */ts/* in most positions except word-initial. English kept the older */t/*.

The split:
- **between or after vowels:** German shows *ss* (after short vowel) or *ß* (after long vowel or diphthong). English shows *t*.
- **word-final or before consonant:** German often shows *z* (pronounced /ts/). English shows *t*.

## Pairs

**water ↔ das Wasser** *(A1)*
Short vowel before *t*, so *ss* in German.
*Das Wasser im Fluss ist kalt.*

**eat ↔ essen** *(A1)*
*Wir essen heute um sieben Uhr.*

**better ↔ besser** *(A1)*
*Heute geht es mir besser.*

**foot ↔ der Fuß** *(A1)*
Long vowel, so *ß* in German.
*Er steht auf einem Fuß.*

**street ↔ die Straße** *(A1)*
Long vowel, so *ß* in German.
*Die Straße ist sehr lang.*

**salt ↔ das Salz** *(A1)*
Word-final after consonant, so *z* in German.
*Die Suppe braucht noch etwas Salz.*

**set ↔ der Satz** *(A1)*
*Satz* primarily means "sentence" in modern German, but the etymological link to *set* is direct.
*Der erste Satz ist oft der schwerste.*

**white ↔ weiß** *(A1, in compound Weißbrot)*
*Anna kauft Weißbrot beim Bäcker.*

**forget ↔ vergessen** *(A2, verb)*
Short vowel before *t*, so *ss* in German.
*Ich habe meine Hausaufgaben vergessen.*

**let ↔ lassen** *(A2, verb)*
Short vowel, so *ss*. Semantic overlap is partial: *lassen* covers "let" plus "leave (behind)."
*Lass mich bitte nicht so lange warten.*

**hot ↔ heiß** *(A2, adjective)*
Long vowel, so *ß*.
*Vorsicht, der Tee ist sehr heiß.*

**white ↔ weiß** *(A2, also color)*
Long vowel + diphthong, so *ß*. (Was in A1 only in the compound *Weißbrot*; now as a color adjective in A2.)
*Sie trägt eine weiße Bluse.*

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

*nut → Nuss*, *bite → beißen*, *sit → sitzen* (also gemination), *out → aus* (related but irregular), *what → was* (irregular), *to → zu* (word-initial *t* shifted to *z* here, exception to the usual word-initial preservation).

## Note on word-initial t

The shift mostly skipped word-initial position — English *take, time, tell, two, ten* stayed as *t*. But a small set of high-frequency words did shift initially, giving us *zwei* (two), *zehn* (ten), *zu* (to), *Zeit* (time, related to *tide*), *Zunge* (tongue). Those need to be memorized rather than predicted.
