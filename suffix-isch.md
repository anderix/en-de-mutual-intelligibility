# Suffix -isch

The classic Madrigal pattern. German *-isch* maps to English *-ic* or *-ical*. Sparse at A1, but explodes at A2 and above as scholarly and technical vocabulary enters.

The etymological connection is shared Greco-Latin borrowing into both languages, mediated by different inflectional layers.

## Pairs

**automatic ↔ automatisch** *(A1, adjective)*
*Die Tür öffnet sich automatisch.*

**foreign ↔ ausländisch** *(A1, derived from Ausland)*
The English cognate of *ausländisch* is *out-landish*, but modern English uses "foreign" instead.
*Er arbeitet für eine ausländische Firma.*

**international ↔ international** *(A1, identical spelling)*
A double-pattern entry: also fits identical-spelling-and-sound. Listed here because the *-al* suffix sits on top of *-tion*, both shared.
*Unsere Schule ist sehr international.*

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

This is the pattern that pays out massively above A1:
*elektrisch / electric*, *historisch / historic*, *politisch / political*, *kritisch / critical*, *praktisch / practical*, *typisch / typical*, *technisch / technical*, *ironisch / ironic*, *organisch / organic*, *pädagogisch / pedagogic*, *philosophisch / philosophical*, *romantisch / romantic*, *fantastisch / fantastic*, *systematisch / systematic*, *symbolisch / symbolic*, *tragisch / tragic*, *exotisch / exotic*, *enthusiastisch / enthusiastic*.

The Madrigal book opens its pattern instruction with this exact mapping and lists dozens of pairs in Chapter 1. Worth pulling them in once we hit A2/B1.

## Note

There's a second use of *-isch* that ISN'T the Latin/Greek *-ic* pattern: it forms adjectives from nouns and place names. *Englisch, Deutsch, Spanisch, Italienisch, Französisch* — all language adjectives. *kindisch* (childish), *neidisch* (envious). For these, the English equivalent is often *-ish* (English/englisch matches both forms exactly).

So the rule has two branches:
- Latin/Greek root + -isch → English -ic / -ical
- Germanic root + -isch → English -ish
