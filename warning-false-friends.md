# Warning: false friends (falsche Freunde)

Words that look like English equivalents but mean something different. These are the highest-cost mistakes a learner can make: you read or hear the German word, your brain inserts the English meaning, and you walk away with the wrong idea — usually without noticing.

For each entry: the German word, what it actually means in German, and what English speakers might assume it means.

## Entries from A1

**also** *(A1, adverb)*
Means **therefore, so** in German. NOT English "also" (which is *auch*).
*Er hat heute frei, also kann er uns helfen.* (He's off today, **so** he can help us.)

**bald** *(A1, adverb)*
Means **soon** in German. NOT English "bald" (hairless), which is *kahl* in German.
*Mach schnell, wir kommen bald an.* (Hurry up, we're arriving **soon**.)

**bekommen** *(A1, verb)*
Means **to receive, to get** in German. NOT English "to become," which is *werden* in German.
*Hast du meinen Brief schon bekommen?* (Have you **received** my letter yet?)

**Brief** *(A1, der Brief)*
Means **letter** (the kind you mail) in German. NOT English "brief" (short or a legal document).
*Der Brief liegt auf deinem Schreibtisch.* (The **letter** is on your desk.)

**Chef** *(A1, der Chef)*
Means **boss** in German. NOT English "chef" (cook), which is *Koch* in German.
*Mein Chef ist heute auf Geschäftsreise.* (My **boss** is on a business trip today.)

**hell** *(A1, adjective)*
Means **bright, light** in German. NOT English "hell" (the underworld), which is *die Hölle* in German.
*Das Zimmer ist sehr hell am Morgen.* (The room is very **bright** in the morning.)

**Herd** *(A1, der Herd)*
Means **stove, cooktop** in German. NOT English "herd" (group of animals), which is *die Herde* in German. Note: *der Herd* is masculine; *die Herde* is feminine.
*Der Herd in der neuen Wohnung ist elektrisch.* (The **stove** in the new apartment is electric.)

**Kind** *(A1, das Kind)*
Means **child** in German. NOT English "kind" (friendly), which is *nett* or *freundlich* in German.
*Mein Kind spielt im Garten.* (My **child** is playing in the garden.)

**lustig** *(A1, adjective)*
Means **funny, amusing** in German. NOT English "lusty" (full of vigor or sexually eager), which carries different connotations entirely.
*Der Film gestern war wirklich lustig.* (The film yesterday was really **funny**.)

**Prospekt** *(A1, der Prospekt)*
Means **brochure, pamphlet** in German. NOT English "prospect" (potential customer or expected outcome).
*Im Prospekt stehen alle Preise.* (All the prices are in the **brochure**.)

**Tisch** *(A1, der Tisch)*
Means **table** in German. NOT English "dish." Both descend from Latin *discus* (disc-shaped plate); German moved the meaning to the piece of furniture, English to the plate of food.
*Das Essen steht schon auf dem Tisch.* (The food is already on the **table**.)

**See** *(A1, der See / die See)*
Gendered ambiguity rather than a false friend, but worth flagging:
- *der See* (masculine) = lake
- *die See* (feminine) = sea
The gender determines the meaning. English *sea* sounds like both.
*Im Sommer schwimmen wir im See.* (In summer we swim in the **lake**.)

**Bank** *(A1, die Bank)*
Two meanings: **bank** (financial) AND **bench** (the seating kind). Context disambiguates.
*Die Bank öffnet erst um neun Uhr.* (The **bank** opens only at nine.)
*Auf der Bank vor dem Haus sitzt meine Großmutter.* (My grandmother sits on the **bench** in front of the house.)

**Handy** *(A1, das Handy)* — see warning-pseudo-anglicisms.md for full treatment
Means **cell phone** in German. NOT English "handy" (useful).

## Entries from A2

**aktuell** *(A2, adjective)*
Means **current, up-to-date** in German. NOT English "actual" (which is *tatsächlich* in German).
*Haben Sie die aktuelle Zeitung?* (Do you have the **current** newspaper?)

**eventuell** *(A2, adverb)*
Means **possibly, maybe** in German. NOT English "eventually" (which is *schließlich* in German).
*Eventuell komme ich später.* (I **might** come later.)

**sympathisch** *(A2, adjective)*
Means **likeable, agreeable** in German. NOT English "sympathetic" (which is *mitfühlend* in German).
*Der neue Kollege ist sehr sympathisch.* (The new colleague is very **likeable**.)

**komisch** *(A2, adjective)* — partial false friend
Means **funny** OR **strange/odd** in German. NOT English "comic" in the narrow sense. Context disambiguates.
*Das Essen schmeckt komisch.* (The food tastes **strange**.)
*Der Film war wirklich komisch.* (The film was really **funny**.)

**fast** *(A2, adverb)* — major false friend
Means **almost, nearly** in German. NOT English "fast" (which is *schnell* in German).
*Ich bin fast fertig.* (I'm **almost** finished.)
This is one of the most common traps for English speakers. A German speaker saying *"Ich bin fast fertig"* is saying "I'm almost done," not "I'm ready fast."

**genial** *(not yet in A2 scope but common)*
Means **brilliant, ingenious** in German. NOT English "genial" (which is *freundlich* in German).

## The reverse direction

A few German words also create traps when English speakers learn the equivalent — *Gift* (German for "poison") looks like English "gift" but isn't; the German for "gift" is *Geschenk* (which is in A1).

**Gift** *(not in A1 directly, but Geschenk is)*
*Gift* in German = poison. *Gift* in English = present. The German for "gift/present" is *das Geschenk*.
*Vielen Dank für das schöne Geschenk!* (Thank you for the lovely **gift**!)

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

The false friend list expands at A2 and B1:
*sensibel* (sensitive, not sensible), *eventuell* (possibly, not eventually), *aktuell* (current, not actually), *sympathisch* (likeable, not sympathetic), *pathetisch* (overly emotional/melodramatic, not pathetic), *konsequent* (consistent, not consequent/important), *Konfession* (religious denomination, not confession), *blamieren* (to embarrass, not to blame), *Rente* (pension, not rent), *Rat* (advice, not rat), *Art* (kind/type, not art), *Mist* (manure/crap, not mist), *Hose* (pants, not hose), *Rock* (skirt, not rock).

## Note

The reason false friends are dangerous is that the brain doesn't flag them. With an unknown word, you stop and look it up. With a familiar-looking word, you autocomplete the meaning and move on. By the time you notice the misunderstanding, you're three sentences past the actual point.

The defense is to keep this list growing and to read it cold every few months. Once you've consciously seen *bekommen ≠ become* a few times, the autopilot stops kicking in.
