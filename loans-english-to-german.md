# English → German loans (Denglisch)

English words absorbed into German, mostly post-1945 and accelerating after 1990. These are direct imports — German treats them as German nouns (capitalized, gendered, declined) but the spelling stays English. Pronunciation is anglicized through a German accent.

This category is where Madrigal's 1966 book has its biggest gap. Most Denglisch is post-Madrigal.

## Pairs

**apartment ↔ das Apartment** *(A1)*
Note: also in *loans-latin-greek-shared.md* since the word existed in Romance languages first. In modern German, it functions as an English loan.
*Das Apartment hat zwei Schlafzimmer.*

**baby ↔ das Baby** *(A1)*
*Das Baby schläft schon.*

**computer ↔ der Computer** *(A1)*
*Mein Computer ist drei Jahre alt.*

**disco ↔ die Disco** *(A1)*
Sometimes spelled *Disko* in German. Both are accepted.
*Die Disco schließt erst um vier Uhr morgens.*

**email ↔ die E-Mail** *(A1)*
Capitalization style varies; *die E-Mail* is the formal Duden form.
*Ich bekomme jeden Tag viele E-Mails.*

**fax ↔ das Fax** *(A1)*
*Dieses Fax ist schon alt.*

**handy ↔ das Handy** *(A1, false friend)*
WARNING: *Handy* in German means cell phone, not "convenient." See warning-pseudo-anglicisms.md.
*Mein Handy hat kaum noch Akku.*

**hobby ↔ das Hobby** *(A1)*
Plural: *die Hobbys* (German typically uses -s for English loans, not -ies).
*Fotografie ist mein neues Hobby.*

**hotel ↔ das Hotel** *(A1, French via English now generic)*
*Das Hotel hat einen schönen Pool.*

**internet ↔ das Internet** *(A1)*
*Das Internet im Café funktioniert nicht.*

**job ↔ der Job** *(A1)*
*Sie sucht einen neuen Job in Berlin.*

**party ↔ die Party** *(A1)*
*Auf der Party gab es gutes Essen.*

**sport ↔ der Sport** *(A1)*
*Sport hält mich gesund.*

**taxi ↔ das Taxi** *(A1)*
*Nehmen wir ein Taxi zum Flughafen?*

**test ↔ der Test** *(A1)*
*Der Test war heute leichter als gestern.*

**ticket ↔ das Ticket** *(A1)*
Note: German also has *die Fahrkarte* and *die Eintrittskarte* for transit and admission tickets respectively. *Ticket* is more general/casual.
*Mein Ticket für das Konzert ist verschwunden.*

## Pairs to expect at higher levels

The Denglisch flood at A2 and above:
*Manager*, *Meeting*, *Trend*, *Marketing*, *Service*, *Online*, *Download*, *Update*, *Software*, *Hardware*, *Website*, *Blog*, *Podcast*, *Workshop*, *Brainstorming*, *Feedback*, *Catering*, *Coaching*, *Lifestyle*, *Smartphone*, *Laptop*, *Streaming*, *Influencer*, *Bestseller*.

Some are already so naturalized they barely register as English: *Boss, Team, Deal, Fan, Star, Show, Look, Style, Mix*. Others remain marked as foreign: *Brainstorming, Smartphone, Streaming*.

## Note on grammar

When German absorbs an English word, three things happen automatically:
1. **It gets a gender.** Usually the gender of the closest German equivalent. *Computer* is *der* because *Rechner* (calculator) is *der*. *E-Mail* is *die* because *Post* is *die*. There are no firm rules; you have to learn each one.
2. **It gets capitalized** (if a noun).
3. **It gets a German plural.** Usually *-s* for English loans (*die Computers, die Hobbys*), not the English *-ies*. Some take German plurals: *die Tests* but *die Manager* (no change in plural).

For verbs: English verbs that enter German get *-en* and conjugate normally. *clicken / klicken* → *ich klicke, du klickst, er klickt*. Past participle: *geklickt*. *downloaden* → *gedownloadet* or *downgeloadet* (both seen in practice).
